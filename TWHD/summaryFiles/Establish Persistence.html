Establishing Persistence in Pentesting Workflow

Introduction

Establishing persistence is a crucial phase in the pentesting workflow that involves ensuring continued access and control over a compromised system. In this phase, penetration testers aim to create backdoors, hidden entry points, or maintain a foothold within the target environment. The objective is to mimic the actions of a real attacker who seeks long-term access without detection.

Objectives

The primary objectives of the Establishing Persistence phase include:

1. Long-Term Access

Ensuring that the penetration tester maintains access to the compromised system even after initial exploitation. This may involve creating user accounts, modifying system configurations, or deploying persistent malware.

2. Subverting Security Measures

Implementing techniques to bypass or subvert security measures such as firewalls, intrusion detection systems (IDS), and antivirus solutions. Establishing persistence allows for the evasion of routine security scans and updates.

3. Avoiding Detection

Implementing stealthy and covert mechanisms to avoid detection by security monitoring tools. This includes hiding the presence of backdoors, disguising malicious activities, and encrypting communication channels.

4. Maintaining Control

Retaining control over the compromised system to facilitate further exploration, data exfiltration, or the execution of additional attack vectors. Establishing persistence is a precursor to subsequent stages in the penetration testing process.

Methods and Techniques

Various methods and techniques are employed during the Establishing Persistence phase:

1. Backdoor Installation

Pentesters may deploy backdoors, which are covert entry points that provide unauthorized access to the system. Backdoors are designed to operate silently and can be activated by the tester at a later stage.

2. Malicious Services

Creating malicious services or processes that run in the background, allowing the tester to connect remotely and execute commands. These services are often configured to start automatically with system boot.

3. Registry Modifications

Modifying registry entries on Windows systems to achieve persistence. This may involve adding entries that launch malicious scripts or executables during system startup.

4. Scheduled Tasks

Creating scheduled tasks or cron jobs on Unix-based systems to execute malicious actions at predefined intervals. This ensures that the pentester maintains control over the system over an extended period.

5. Rootkit Deployment

Deploying rootkits to conceal the presence of malicious activities. Rootkits are designed to manipulate system functions and evade detection by security software.

Ethical Considerations

While establishing persistence is a necessary aspect of penetration testing, ethical considerations are paramount. Pentesters must operate within the confines of legal and ethical standards, ensuring that their actions do not cause harm to the systems, networks, or data being assessed.

Conclusion

The Establishing Persistence phase is a critical component of the pentesting process, contributing to a holistic evaluation of an organization's security posture. By simulating the actions of real adversaries, penetration testers assist in identifying and addressing vulnerabilities that could be exploited for long-term unauthorized access.
